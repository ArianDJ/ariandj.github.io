Introduction

Pythagoras' theorem is one of the most fundamental concepts in mathematics, with applications ranging from basic geometry to advanced engineering. Named after the Greek mathematician Pythagoras, this theorem establishes the relationship between the sides of a right triangle.

The Theorem

In a right triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares of the lengths of the other two sides.

\(AB^2 + AC^2 = BC^2\)

where \(AB\) and \(AC\) are the lengths of the legs (the sides adjacent to the right angle), and \(BC\) is the length of the hypotenuse (the side opposite to the right angle).

IMPORTANT: A SKETCH SHOULD ALWAYS BE MADE when solving problems with Pythagoras' theorem!

\(Horizontal\ distance = 795m\)

\(Actual\ length = \sqrt{302^2 + 795^2} = \sqrt{91204 + 632025} = \sqrt{723229} \approx 850.4m\)

Calculate Lengths

One of the most common applications of Pythagoras' Theorem is calculating the unknown length of a side in a right triangle. Follow this systematic approach to solve such problems effectively.

ALWAYS MAKE A SKETCH! Creating a proper sketch is the first and most crucial step in applying Pythagoras' theorem.

Steps to Calculate Lengths:

Step 1: Sketch a right triangle

Draw a right triangle, label the vertices as A, B, and C, and add the known lengths. Ensure you're using consistent units for all measurements.

Step 2: Write the Pythagorean theorem in letters only

Express the relationship using the vertices: \(AB^2 + AC^2 = BC^2\) where \(BC\) is the hypotenuse.

Step 3: Replace the letters with known values

Substitute the known values into the formula, leaving any unknown values as variables.

Step 4: Solve using the Balance Method

Use algebraic manipulation to isolate the unknown value, taking the square root as needed.

Note: You may skip step 1 if the assignment already provides a correct drawing with both letters and numbers, such as in assignment 11.

The Pythagorean theorem can also be used to determine whether a triangle is a right triangle (rectangular). If the sum of the squares of two sides equals the square of the third side, the triangle contains a right angle.

ALWAYS MAKE A SKETCH! When checking if a triangle is rectangular, start by drawing the triangle with clearly labeled vertices.

Step 1: Sketch the triangle

Space Diagonals in 3D Cuboids

A space diagonal is a line segment that connects two vertices of a three-dimensional shape that are not on the same face. In this section, we'll explore how to calculate space diagonals in cuboids using the Pythagorean theorem twice.

ALWAYS MAKE A SKETCH! When working with 3D shapes, a clear 2D sketch is essential for visualizing the space diagonal problem.

What is a Space Diagonal?

A space diagonal in a cuboid (rectangular prism) is a line segment that connects two vertices at opposite corners of the shape. Unlike face diagonals (which lie on a single face), a space diagonal passes through the interior of the cuboid.

For a cuboid with dimensions length (l), width (w), and height (h):

  • A face diagonal connects opposite corners on the same face
  • A space diagonal connects vertices at opposite ends of the cuboid
  • The length of the space diagonal can be calculated as: \(\sqrt{l^2 + w^2 + h^2}\)

Figure: A cuboid with its space diagonal (purple line) connecting vertices A and B

Calculating a Space Diagonal: Step-by-Step Method

To calculate the space diagonal in a cuboid, we apply the Pythagorean theorem twice in sequence:

Step 1: Sketch the cuboid in 2D

Create a 2D sketch of the cuboid, labeling the vertices clearly with capital letters. Mark the dimensions: length (l), width (w), and height (h).

Step 2: Find a face diagonal first

Use the Pythagorean theorem to calculate the diagonal of one of the faces (usually the base). If the base has length l and width w, then:

Face diagonal¬≤ = l¬≤ + w¬≤

Step 3: Use the face diagonal to find the space diagonal

Apply the Pythagorean theorem again, using the face diagonal and the height:

Space diagonal¬≤ = (Face diagonal)¬≤ + h¬≤

Space diagonal¬≤ = (l¬≤ + w¬≤) + h¬≤ = l¬≤ + w¬≤ + h¬≤

Step 4: Follow the standard formatting

Mathematical Expression:

The space diagonal of a cuboid with dimensions l, w, and h is given by:

\(d = \sqrt{l^2 + w^2 + h^2}\)

This formula is derived by applying the Pythagorean theorem twice: first to find a face diagonal, then to find the space diagonal.
