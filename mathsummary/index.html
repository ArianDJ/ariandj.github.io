Pythagoras' Theorem

Interactive Learning Guide

Introduction

Pythagoras' theorem is one of the most fundamental concepts in mathematics, with applications ranging from basic geometry to advanced engineering. Named after the Greek mathematician Pythagoras, this theorem establishes the relationship between the sides of a right triangle.

The Theorem

In a right triangle, the square of the length of the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares of the lengths of the other two sides.

\(AB^2 + AC^2 = BC^2\)

where \(AB\) and \(AC\) are the lengths of the legs (the sides adjacent to the right angle), and \(BC\) is the length of the hypotenuse (the side opposite to the right angle).

IMPORTANT: A SKETCH SHOULD ALWAYS BE MADE when solving problems with Pythagoras' theorem!

Real-World Application

Consider the Fløibanen funicular in Bergen, Norway. It travels from a height of 18 meters to 320 meters, covering a horizontal distance of 795 meters. Using Pythagoras' theorem, we can calculate the actual length of the funicular track.

Calculation:

\(Height\ difference = 320m - 18m = 302m\)

\(Horizontal\ distance = 795m\)

\(Actual\ length = \sqrt{302^2 + 795^2} = \sqrt{91204 + 632025} = \sqrt{723229} \approx 850.4m\)

Calculate Lengths

One of the most common applications of Pythagoras' Theorem is calculating the unknown length of a side in a right triangle. Follow this systematic approach to solve such problems effectively.

ALWAYS MAKE A SKETCH! Creating a proper sketch is the first and most crucial step in applying Pythagoras' theorem.

Steps to Calculate Lengths:

Step 1: Sketch a right triangle

Draw a right triangle, label the vertices as A, B, and C, and add the known lengths. Ensure you're using consistent units for all measurements.

Step 2: Write the Pythagorean theorem in letters only

Express the relationship using the vertices: \(AB^2 + AC^2 = BC^2\) where \(BC\) is the hypotenuse.

Step 3: Replace the letters with known values

Substitute the known values into the formula, leaving any unknown values as variables.

Step 4: Solve using the Balance Method

Use algebraic manipulation to isolate the unknown value, taking the square root as needed.

Note: You may skip step 1 if the assignment already provides a correct drawing with both letters and numbers, such as in assignment 11.

Example Problem

A ladder is leaning against a wall. The foot of the ladder is 3 meters from the wall, and the ladder reaches a height of 4 meters on the wall. What is the length of the ladder?

Solution:

Step 1: Sketch the right triangle

- Let's label vertices as A, B, and C

- The distance from the wall (AB) = 3 meters

- The height up the wall (AC) = 4 meters

- The length of the ladder (BC) = ?

Step 2: Write the Pythagorean theorem

AB² + AC² = BC²

Step 3: Replace with known values

3² + 4² = BC²

9 + 16 = BC²

25 = BC²

Step 4: Solve for BC

BC = √25 = 5

Answer: The ladder is 5 meters long.

Check If A Triangle Is Rectangular

The Pythagorean theorem can also be used to determine whether a triangle is a right triangle (rectangular). If the sum of the squares of two sides equals the square of the third side, the triangle contains a right angle.

ALWAYS MAKE A SKETCH! When checking if a triangle is rectangular, start by drawing the triangle with clearly labeled vertices.

Steps to Check If a Triangle is Rectangular:

Step 1: Sketch the triangle

Draw the triangle, label the vertices as A, B, and C, and add the known lengths. Ensure you're using consistent units.

Step 2a: Apply the Pythagorean theorem to the sides

Write down the Pythagorean theorem using letters (AB² + AC² = BC²), then substitute the actual values and calculate.

Step 2b: Apply the Pythagorean theorem to the hypotenuse

Identify the longest side as a potential hypotenuse, then check if AB² + AC² = BC².

Step 3: Write your conclusion

State whether the triangle is rectangular (right-angled) or not, using a ✓ symbol if it is.

Difference between study objectives 1 and 2:

For objective 1 (page 57), we use the theorem to find an unknown side: AB² + AC² = BC²

For example: 4² + AC² = 5², so we solve for AC.

For objective 2 (page 65), we verify if a triangle is rectangular by checking whether the equation holds true:

AB² + AC² = 3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25 and BC² = 5² = 25

Since 25 = 25, the triangle is rectangular (contains a right angle).

Example Problem

Determine whether a triangle with sides of lengths 7, 24, and 25 is a right triangle.

Solution:

Step 1: Sketch the triangle and label vertices

Step 2: Identify the longest side as the potential hypotenuse.

BC = 25 is the longest side, so it's our potential hypotenuse.

Step 2: Apply the Pythagorean theorem.

For a right triangle: AB² + AC² = BC²

Check if 24² + 7² = 25²

24² + 7² = 576 + 49 = 625

25² = 625

Step 3: Draw a conclusion.

Since AB² + AC² = BC², this triangle is rectangular ✓

Triangle Sketches

Proper sketching of triangles is essential for visualizing and solving problems involving Pythagoras' theorem. This section covers guidelines for creating effective sketches in both 2D and 3D contexts.

ALWAYS MAKE A SKETCH! Sketching is absolutely essential when solving geometric problems, especially in 3D situations.

Key Sketching Guidelines:

Guideline 1: Add a guideline if there is no right angle (page 67)

When drawing a triangle without a right angle, include auxiliary lines to form right triangles where Pythagoras' theorem can be applied.

Guideline 2: ALWAYS use a 2D sketch in a 3D situation (page 74)

When dealing with three-dimensional problems, create a two-dimensional sketch that accurately represents the relationship you need to analyze.

Interactive Sketching Demonstrations

Adding Guidelines to Non-Right Triangles

In this example, we have a triangle without a right angle. To apply Pythagoras' theorem:

  1. Draw a height line from vertex C to the opposite side AB
  2. This creates two right triangles: ACD and BCD
  3. Now Pythagoras' theorem can be applied to each of these right triangles

This technique allows us to solve problems involving non-right triangles by decomposing them into right triangles.

2D Sketches in 3D Situations

When dealing with 3D objects (like cubes, pyramids, etc.), follow these steps:

  1. Identify the specific distances you need to find (like the body diagonal AB in this cube)
  2. Draw a 2D representation that shows those distances clearly
  3. Label all relevant points using capital letters (A, B, C, etc.)
  4. Apply Pythagoras' theorem to the resulting right triangles

This approach simplifies complex 3D problems by isolating the specific triangles needed for calculation.

Example Problem: 3D Application

Find the distance from one corner of a cube with side length 5 cm to the opposite corner (the body diagonal).

Solution:

Step 1: Create a 2D representation of the problem

Step 1: Find the face diagonal using Pythagoras

Label the corners of interest as A, B, and intermediate point C

For face diagonal AC:

AC² = 5² + 5² = 25 + 25 = 50

AC = √50 = 5√2 cm

Step 2: Now use the face diagonal to find body diagonal

The body diagonal AB can be found using:

AB² = AC² + 5²

AB² = (5√2)² + 5²

AB² = 50 + 25 = 75

AB = √75 = 5√3 cm ≈ 8.66 cm

Practice Problems

Problem 1:

In an equilateral triangle with side length 10 cm, find the height of the triangle.

Step 1: Draw the equilateral triangle with a height line

Step 2: Apply Pythagoras' theorem to the right triangle

This creates a right triangle with:

- Hypotenuse = 10 cm (side of the equilateral triangle)

- Base = 5 cm (half of the side AB)

- Height = h (what we're solving for)

Step 3: Apply Pythagoras' theorem

h² + 5² = 10²

h² + 25 = 100

h² = 75

h = √75 = 5√3 ≈ 8.66 cm

Problem 2:

Find the distance from one corner of a rectangular box with dimensions 3 cm × 4 cm × 5 cm to the opposite corner.

Step 1: Draw a 2D representation of the box and diagonal

Step 1: Find the diagonal of the base rectangle

diagonal² = 3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25

diagonal = 5 cm

Step 2: Find the space diagonal using Pythagoras again

space diagonal² = base diagonal² + height²

space diagonal² = 5² + 5² = 25 + 25 = 50

space diagonal = √50 = 5√2 ≈ 7.07 cm
